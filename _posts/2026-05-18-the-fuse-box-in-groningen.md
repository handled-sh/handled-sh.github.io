Hugo called from Groningen on a Sunday evening. Not a video call, just a voice message: the power in his apartment had gone out. Not the whole building, just his place. Everything dark.

He's 20 and living on his own for the first time, in an apartment he moved into just before Christmas. He's good at a lot of things. Dutch fuse boxes are not yet on that list.

From Dubai, I pulled up the layout of a standard Dutch meterkast -- the electrical panel -- and walked him through it step by step. Which switches to check, which to flip, in what order. It took about eight minutes. The lights came back on.

He sent back a thumbs up. Conversation over.

I've been thinking about that exchange since. Not because it was dramatic, it wasn't. But because of how normal it felt. Two people on opposite ends of a time zone, sorting out a practical problem in a few minutes.

That is what this technology is supposed to do. Not replace people. Not impress anyone. Just quietly close the distance between where you are and what you need to know.

Hugo is studying at the University of Groningen. He manages his own schedule, his own apartment, his own life. He doesn't need me to hold his hand. But occasionally a fuse trips and it's a Sunday evening and Google gives you twelve links when you just want one clear answer.

That's the gap worth closing. Not with a lecture. With an answer.

The fuse box is fine. So is he.
